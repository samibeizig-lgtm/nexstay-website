Getting Your Airbnb Ready for the Tunisian Summer: What Owners Always Put Off Until It's Too Late

The Tunisian summer is unforgiving if your Airbnb isn't ready. Here's what property owners consistently forget before high season — and how to fix it before the complaints start.

Published on June 3, 2026 · 6 min read · By Nexstay Conciergerie

Most owners start thinking about summer preparation somewhere in late June. A quick visit to the property, a check that the AC is still working, maybe a new set of towels. And then July arrives, guests arrive, and things that seemed fine in winter turn out to be completely inadequate under 40-degree heat. The gap between "probably fine" and "actually ready" is where one-star reviews are born.

Here's the thing: guests in Tunisia's high season book from April onwards. Sometimes March. By the time you're doing your June walkthrough, a significant portion of your summer calendar is already committed. The preparation window is earlier than you think, and the cost of getting it wrong isn't just a bad review — it's that review sitting on your listing permanently, visible to every future guest who searches for properties in La Marsa or Hammamet in July.

Air conditioning — the one thing you cannot get away with neglecting

Every Tunisian owner will tell you their AC works fine. And technically, it probably does — it turns on, it blows air, it makes the room cooler than it was. But that's not the standard guests judge you by at 11:30pm on a 36-degree July night when the unit starts making a grinding noise and the temperature in the bedroom won't drop below 28 degrees.

The filters. That's almost always where the problem starts. Clogged filters from a year of dust, particularly in properties near the coast where salt air adds to the buildup, will cut an AC unit's efficiency by 30 to 40 percent. The unit runs constantly, the room barely cools, and the electricity bill climbs. Having the filters cleaned — or replaced — before June is not optional. It's the baseline.

Beyond filters: the remote control. Yes, the remote control. It sounds trivial until a guest arrives after a long flight, can't find fresh batteries in a property they don't know, and spends their first night sweating because the unit won't respond. Leave spare AA batteries somewhere obvious. Check that the remote's settings haven't been locked on an odd mode from the last guest. These are five-minute jobs that prevent genuinely angry messages at midnight.

Bedding in summer — "still usable" isn't good enough

Tunisia in July is not Switzerland in July. A fitted sheet that performed perfectly fine in February becomes a genuine problem when it's slightly yellowed, slightly musty from months in storage, and has that particular smell of a property that's been closed up since October. Guests notice. They notice immediately, before they've even unpacked.

Light, fresh linen is a summer requirement, not a luxury upgrade. Cotton that breathes. Pillowcases without mystery stains. Mattress protectors that don't crinkle when someone moves. The investment is modest — a full set of summer bedding for a standard double room is 80 to 120 DT — and the return in guest satisfaction is disproportionate. Nobody writes a glowing review about the thread count. But they absolutely mention damp or stuffy bedding in a negative one.

Mosquito protection — the detail that destroys a night

Tunisia's coastal properties in summer are beautiful. They're also, depending on proximity to standing water, gardens, or certain neighborhoods, mosquito territory. This is not a seasonal surprise. It happens every year. And yet it remains one of the most consistently overlooked items in pre-summer preparation.

Window mosquito nets that are torn or missing gaps are useless. A guest who opens the window at 2am to let in the breeze — because the AC was struggling, perhaps — and then spends the rest of the night listening to buzzing is not going to sleep well. More importantly, they're going to mention it. The review will say something like "wonderful property but the nights were ruined by mosquitoes" and that sentence will cost you bookings for seasons to come.

The fix is straightforward: check every window screen before June. Replace anything that's compromised. Leave a can of repellent spray somewhere visible, or a plug-in repellent device already installed. The cost is minimal. The goodwill it generates — or rather, the catastrophe it prevents — is not.

What guests actually ask about in Tunisian summers

After managing properties across the northern suburbs for several seasons, we've noticed patterns in what guests specifically inquire about before booking. Covered or shaded parking is one of the most common questions we receive for La Marsa and Sidi Bou Said properties — not because guests are precious about their cars, but because getting into a vehicle that's been sitting in direct sun for six hours in July is genuinely unpleasant, and they know it.

Cold water in the fridge on arrival is another. A simple gesture — a few bottles left in the refrigerator — that lands disproportionately well when someone has just spent two hours navigating Tunis Carthage airport and the motorway in the heat. Shutters that actually block afternoon light matter too: if the volets are decorative rather than functional, the bedroom becomes a greenhouse by 3pm regardless of what the AC is doing.

Reliable WiFi for working from the terrace comes up more than you'd expect. Remote workers traveling through Tunisia increasingly choose apartments over hotels precisely because of the flexibility — and a connection that drops every fifteen minutes defeats the purpose entirely. Test it from the outdoor spaces before the season starts.

"The difference between a livable property and a memorable one isn't the view or the square footage. It's the ten small decisions that tell a guest someone actually thought about their comfort."

The solar water heater problem nobody explains to guests

This one is specific to Tunisia, and it catches international guests completely off guard. In peak summer, a solar water heater on a south-facing roof can produce scalding water in the morning — genuinely dangerous, not just hot. By late evening, depending on the installation and the previous day's usage, it might be lukewarm or cold.

Guests who've never encountered this have no framework for it. They turn on the shower at 7am expecting normal hot water and get something that could strip paint. Then they message you in alarm, assuming something is broken. Or they don't message you, burn themselves mildly, and write about it in the review. Neither outcome is good.

The solution isn't to change the heating system — it's to explain it. Your welcome guide needs a clear paragraph on how the solar heater works: morning water will be very hot (add cold to balance it), evening water may be cooler, here's what to do if you want a hot shower at night. Thirty seconds of reading prevents hours of confusion and complaint.

When to actually do the checks — and why June is already too late

The answer is May. Ideally late April. Not because there's some arbitrary rule, but because of a very practical constraint: every competent plumber, electrician, and AC technician in greater Tunis is available in April and essentially unreachable in July. They're servicing properties all along the coast, handling emergency calls from panicked owners, and working backlogs that extend weeks.

Book your AC service in late April. Have the plumber check the solar heater connections and the water pressure in May. Get the electrician to look at the fuse box and any exterior lighting before the season starts. These are not dramatic interventions — an annual AC service takes ninety minutes, costs around 80 to 120 DT depending on the number of units, and virtually eliminates the risk of a mid-August breakdown. Compare that to the cost of an emergency call on a Saturday in July, assuming you can even find someone available.

A well-prepared property going into summer doesn't just avoid complaints. It generates the kind of specific, enthusiastic five-star reviews that mention the cool apartment and the clean sheets and the cold water waiting in the fridge — the reviews that do your marketing for the next twelve months, long after the season is over.
