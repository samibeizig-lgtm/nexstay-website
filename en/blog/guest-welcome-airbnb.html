Guest Experience

Welcoming Airbnb Guests in Tunisia: Why the First Ten Minutes Determine Your Review

The review a guest leaves on your Airbnb forms in the first ten minutes after arrival. Here's what actually happens in that window — and how not to waste it.

Published on June 17, 2026 · 8 min read · By Nexstay Conciergerie

The Airbnb review a guest leaves doesn't form at the end of their stay. It forms in the first ten minutes. Before they've opened their suitcase. Before they've tested the shower or tried the WiFi or discovered whether the kitchen has a decent knife. The smell of the property as they open the door. The temperature. Whether the lights come on easily. Whether the place feels like someone was expecting them or like they're the first person to enter since the last checkout was rushed through.

These first moments create an impression that's extraordinarily difficult to dislodge. A guest who arrives to a hot, stuffy apartment with a slightly stale smell and no clear instructions will carry that initial unease through the entire stay — even if everything else goes smoothly. Conversely, a guest who walks into a cool, fresh, well-prepared space will filter minor imperfections through a much more forgiving lens. The first ten minutes set the emotional baseline for everything that follows.

The smell — the detail nobody talks about

A property can be genuinely clean — scrubbed floors, fresh linen, no visible dust — and still smell wrong. In Tunisia, especially in summer, a property that's been closed for even a few days accumulates a particular kind of heat-and-humidity smell that's almost impossible to describe but instantly recognizable. It's not dirty. It's just stale. And it hits you the moment you cross the threshold.

Airing a property out for at least thirty minutes before guest arrival isn't optional — it's a baseline requirement. Open every window in sequence to create a cross-draft, not just one window that creates a pocket of stagnant air. If the property has been closed for a week or more, start the airing process an hour before arrival. Particularly in coastal areas around La Marsa or Gammarth, where sea humidity accumulates, this makes a substantial difference.

A subtle, clean scent — a diffuser with a light eucalyptus or linen fragrance, not the kind of heavy air freshener that itself signals something being masked — finishes the job. The goal isn't perfume. It's the neutral freshness of a place that's been actively prepared.

The arrival temperature

Walking into an apartment that's sitting at 36 degrees because the owner left the AC off to "save electricity" — that's the fastest way to have an irritable guest for the next 48 hours. The heat hits them at the door. They immediately form an association: this place is hot. The AC might fix it over the next hour, but the association has already been made.

The air conditioning needs to be running at least an hour before check-in. For a standard property in the greater Tunis area, that means the apartment should be at a comfortable temperature by the time guests arrive — not in the process of cooling down. The marginal cost of running an AC unit for an extra hour is somewhere around 1 to 1.5 DT, depending on the unit's efficiency. Compare that to the cost of a three-star review that sits visible on your listing for the next three years. There's no calculation to do. Run the AC.

The welcome guide — not a list of rules, an actual guide

There's a meaningful difference between a welcome guide and a list of house rules, and most owners produce the latter while thinking it's the former. Rules feel like a landlord talking down to a tenant. A guide feels like a knowledgeable friend who knows the neighborhood.

The difference is concrete. Rules: "No smoking inside. No noise after 10pm. Do not leave the air conditioning on when you go out." Guide: "WiFi password is Nexstay-Home01, there's a sticker on the router in the living room. The best coffee in the area is three minutes on foot — turn left out of the building, it's the café with the blue sign, they do an excellent brick au thon in the mornings. For taxis in this area, Bolt works reliably; InDriver is also good and usually slightly cheaper. There's a small supermarket open until 9pm on the street behind the building."

Which of those two makes a guest feel welcomed? The guide acknowledges that they're strangers in an unfamiliar place and offers genuinely useful orientation. It also signals that the host pays attention — which is exactly the quality that generates five-star reviews mentioning "thoughtful host" and "felt right at home."

"A host knows the neighborhood. A landlord knows the lease. The review a guest leaves is a direct reflection of which one they felt they were dealing with."

Key handover — in person or automated?

Both approaches work. Neither is inherently superior. What matters is execution.

In-person handover has an irreplaceable advantage: you can explain the property's quirks directly. The solar water heater and its morning-versus-evening behavior. The temperamental washing machine that needs its drum door pressed firmly before the cycle starts. How to reach the building caretaker if anything comes up. These aren't things you want guests discovering alone at 8pm with no context.

A key lockbox is perfectly adequate for late arrivals — a guest landing at 11:30pm from Paris or Marseille doesn't need a full property tour, they need to get in and sleep. But the instructions for a lockbox need to be sent at least 24 hours before arrival, not 40 minutes before they show up at the door. "I sent the code earlier" is not reassuring to someone standing on a dark street in an unfamiliar neighborhood trying to read a WhatsApp message on a phone with 8% battery. Send the code the night before. Confirm it the morning of. Send it again three hours before check-in.

What international guests don't expect — but remember

A cold bottle of water in the fridge. A small dish of dates or whatever fruit is in season — Tunisian figs in late summer, clementines in autumn, a few briqs wrapped in foil if you want to go further. A neighborhood map, ideally hand-drawn or at least personally annotated, with three or four addresses written in your handwriting. Not a printed tourist brochure. Something that looks like a personal recommendation.

These touches collectively cost less than 5 DT. Their impact on reviews is disproportionate and consistent. The specific mentions appear regularly in five-star reviews for properties that include them: "warm welcome," "they thought of everything," "arrived to cold water and dates, what a lovely gesture." It's not the dates themselves — it's what they communicate. That someone thought about this guest specifically, before they arrived.

For international visitors, this kind of welcome is also a first encounter with Tunisian hospitality. Many arrive with limited experience of the country. The arrival experience at your property shapes their entire perception of Tunisia. That's a real opportunity, and most owners leave it completely unused.

Late arrivals — the reality nobody wants to deal with

A flight from Lyon lands at 11:20pm. There's a delay on the A1 motorway. The guest calls at 12:45am to say they're ten minutes away. This is not an exceptional scenario — it's a routine one for anyone managing short-term rentals with international guests. If you're handling arrivals yourself, you need to genuinely accept this reality, or you need to delegate it.

A guest left standing outside a locked door at 1am — because the host stopped answering after midnight, or because the lockbox code was sent to the wrong number, or because no one had thought through what happens in case of delay — does not leave five stars. It doesn't matter how beautiful the apartment is. The experience of standing in the dark, exhausted, with luggage, not knowing how to get in, creates a feeling of abandonment that no amount of nice décor will overcome.

Late arrivals aren't a problem to solve. They're a condition of the business. Either you build a system that handles them gracefully — lockbox with clear instructions, a person available by phone at all hours, a protocol for delays — or you partner with someone who does. There's no middle ground that works consistently.

Welcoming a guest isn't a logistical formality that precedes the stay. It's the beginning of the stay. It's the moment when a stranger who found your listing online, sent you money, got on a plane, navigated a foreign airport, and made their way to your address — that moment when they either feel they made a good choice, or start to wonder. The review they'll leave in three days starts exactly here, at the door, in the first ten minutes. That window is yours to use or waste.
