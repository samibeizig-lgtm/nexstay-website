Listing & Visibility

What Your Airbnb Photos Are Really Saying — And Why They're Costing You Bookings Without You Knowing

Your Airbnb cover photo gets judged in under 3 seconds. Here's what makes a guest click — or scroll past — and how to improve your photos without hiring a professional.

Published on June 10, 2026 · 7 min read · By Nexstay Conciergerie

On Airbnb, a guest spends less than four seconds looking at your thumbnail before deciding whether to click or keep scrolling. Four seconds. Your description, your amenity list, your carefully worded cancellation policy — none of that matters if the first image doesn't catch their eye. And yet the photos are consistently the last thing owners think about when setting up a listing, treated as administrative paperwork rather than the most important marketing asset they have.

The uncomfortable truth is that most Airbnb photos in Tunisia are actively losing bookings. Not because properties are bad — many are genuinely lovely — but because the photos don't show it. A beautiful apartment in La Marsa with a sea view and a well-designed living room can look depressing and cramped if it's shot at the wrong time of day, from the wrong angle, with the curtains closed and the ceiling light on. Which is exactly how most of these photos are taken.

The first photo mistakes — more common than you'd think

The kitchen with dishes in the sink. The bedroom shot from the doorway, capturing most of the corridor. The living room photographed at 3pm with the curtains pulled because the owner thought the outdoor glare would be a problem. The bathroom taken from a standing position in the middle of the room, showing the ceiling fan prominently and the mirror catching the photographer's reflection.

These aren't hypothetical examples. They appear in real listings, on properties that are otherwise well-maintained and well-reviewed. The photos communicate things the owner doesn't intend: that the property is messy, that it's dark, that no one put any thought into presenting it. And once a guest forms that impression — in under four seconds, remember — they've already scrolled to the next listing.

The kitchen mistake is particularly damaging because kitchens signal cleanliness. A single unwashed glass on the counter is enough to make a guest wonder what the rest of the property looks like. Before shooting, every surface needs to be clear. That's not about deception — it's about showing the property at its actual best.

Natural light isn't "nicer" — it's mandatory

There's a persistent belief among owners that photographs taken with interior lights on look warmer and more inviting. They don't. They look like hotel corridors in 2003. Artificial lighting turns walls yellow, flattens textures, and creates harsh shadows that make even large rooms look compressed. Combine that with closed curtains and you have a photo that makes a sunny apartment look like a basement.

The rule is simple: open everything, and shoot in the morning. In winter, 9 to 11am is ideal — the sun is up but still low enough to produce warm, angled light rather than harsh overhead glare. In summer, you need to start earlier: 7 to 9am, before the light gets punishing. By 10am in July, direct sun through Tunisian windows creates blown-out patches that no phone camera can handle gracefully.

Natural light also does something artificial light can't: it makes colors accurate. The terracotta floor tiles, the white walls, the blue-painted shutters that look so good in person — these details disappear under fluorescent or warm-tone bulbs but sing in morning light. You don't need to do anything special to capture them. Just open the windows, pull back the curtains, and wait for the right hour.

The order of photos matters as much as the photos themselves

Guests scroll through a listing's images in a specific psychological sequence. They want an overview first — the living room or the main communal space, something that tells them the scale and feel of the place. Then the bedroom, because that's where they'll be sleeping. Then the bathroom, quickly but necessarily. Then the kitchen. Then anything outside — a terrace, a garden, a sea view if there is one.

What many owners do instead: lead with the street exterior, follow it with the 1980s blue-tiled bathroom, then the hallway, then a detail shot of a decorative lamp, and finally — on photo seven or eight — the main bedroom. By which point the guest has already closed the listing.

The sequencing is a deliberate editorial choice. Think about what you'd want to show someone who had thirty seconds to decide whether to stay in your home. That's your photo order. Lead with your strongest image, which is almost always the most spacious, best-lit room in the property. If you have a terrace with a view, put it second or third — not fourteenth.

"Your cover photo is a book cover. You're not obligated to show the best chapter first — you're obligated to make someone want to open the book."

What a phone does well — and where it falls short

A recent iPhone or high-end Android takes excellent photos in good natural light. For a property priced at 100 to 140 DT per night, phone photography done carefully and at the right time of day is perfectly adequate. The images won't be as polished as professional shots, but they'll be honest and clear, which is what matters most.

Where phones struggle: wide angles without distortion. The ultra-wide lens on most smartphones produces that characteristic barrel effect where walls curve and rooms look vaguely like the inside of a bowl. For small rooms, this often makes them look bigger than they are — which sounds appealing until guests arrive and feel misled. For large rooms, it introduces a fisheye quality that looks amateurish. Use the standard lens for most interior shots and move further back if you need more of the room in frame.

Phones also struggle with color accuracy in low light, and with capturing small rooms completely without distortion. If your property commands more than 150 DT per night in high season, a professional photographer is worth the investment — typically 300 to 500 DT for a full session, paid once, valid for the lifetime of the listing. The return on that investment, measured in bookings won from better presentation, is usually recovered within a month or two of the peak season.

The staging details that change everything — without costing much

You don't need to redecorate. You need to present what's already there as well as it can be presented. Folded towels in a neat stack on the bathroom shelf rather than hanging off the radiator. Cushions arranged on the sofa rather than piled in a corner from the last guest. A green plant on the kitchen counter — a real one, or a convincing artificial one, not a dry, wilted thing that's been ignored for six months.

For properties with a terrace or outdoor space, this is where staging pays off most dramatically. A small table set with two glasses and something seasonal — a glass of water with fresh mint, a bowl of dates, a single flower from the garden — transforms a bare concrete slab into an aspirational space. It doesn't need to be elaborate. It needs to look like someone has thought about it, even briefly.

None of these things cost more than 20 DT, if anything at all. But they shift perception completely. Not just in photos — also in the review a guest leaves when they arrive and find a property that actually looks like its listing.

Photos aren't paperwork. They're your pitch. On a platform where hundreds of owners are essentially offering the same thing — a comfortable place to sleep in a good location — the visual story you tell is often the only real differentiator you have. A property that looks considered and cared-for in photos will attract guests who value consideration and care. That's the guest who leaves five stars and comes back next year.
